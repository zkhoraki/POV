Period and the Menstrual Cycle


Periods or menstruation is normal vaginal bleeding that occurs as part of a woman's monthly cycle. Once a month, a woman's body prepares for pregnancy, and if no pregnancy occurs then the uterus, also known as the womb, sheds its lining. The menstrual blood is partly tissue and partly blood from inside the uterus, and it flows out of the body through the vagina. Periods last around 2-7 days, and the average age that girls start their period is 12 years.


The menstrual cycle is the time from the first day of a woman's period, to the day before their next period. The length of the menstrual cycle varies from woman to woman, but the average length of the menstrual cycle is 28 days.

What is Period Poverty?


Period Poverty is the lack of access to menstrual products and menstrual health education, because of financial constraints and the stigma behind periods. Period poverty affects many women across the United States as well as the world. Period products in most states in the U.S. are taxed as a luxury product, when in reality it is natural.


Health issues caused by period poverty

Period poverty can lead to the many health issues. Without access to menstrual products, women may use unsanitary period products, like dirty rags, instead during their period. This can cause infections and other medical problems. To add on, women may skip meals or take medication in order to draw out their period, and this can lead to anemia and create eating disorders. Additionaly, period poverty can also negatively affect the mental health of women.


How education is affected

Women who cannot afford period products may not attend school, because they do not want to risk bleeding through their clothes. They may also fear being teased, because of the negative stigma about periods.


How physical activity is affected

Women who can not afford menstrual products may be embarresssed to participate in sports, because they may not want to get changed or they may be worried about leaking. This is a problem, because excersize is essential for keeping our lungs and heart healthy. Excersizing also reduces stress levels and can release endorphins. Therefore, women who miss out on sports/excersizing because of period poverty may be at risk for poor health in the future.

Statistics


In the United Kingdom, during the pandemic, more than a third of 14-21 year old girls have struggled to access or afford menstrual products

A 2019 study in Missouri, US, found that nearly 64% of women were not able to afford period products in 2018.

1 in 5 girls in the US reported to have missed school, because they didn't have access to menstrual products.

A UNESCO report estimates that 1 in 10 girls in Sub-Saharan Africa miss school because of their periods

In Kenya, 65% of girls and women are not able to afford period products

A 2020 study, from New Zealand, found that more than 90,000 school girls stay home from school, because they cannot afford period products.

The fight against period poverty



  • India, Kenya, and Lebonan have abolished taxes on menstrual products

  • Kenya became the first country to create a national "Menstrual Hygiene Management Policy"

  • 30 states, in the US, still tax menstrual products

  • 4 states in the United States require public schools to provide free menstrual products to students. These 4 states are: California, Illinois, New Hampshire, New York.

  • New York also require prisons/detention facilities and city shelters to provide free menstrual products.

  • In 2018, Scotland became the first country to make menstrual products free to students. Later in 2020, Scotland made menstrual products free to everyone who needs them. Then in 2022, Scotland became the first country to make menstrual products free to everyone.

  • In 2020, England made menstrual products free for all students
